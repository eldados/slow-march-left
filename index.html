The Slow March of the Left

Understanding Institutional Shift in Australia

What is the "Slow March of the Left"?

The “slow march of the Left” refers to the gradual and strategic spread of progressive or leftist ideologies through society’s core institutions—education, media, bureaucracy, law, entertainment, and even sport. The term draws on the Marxist thinker Antonio Gramsci’s theory that cultural power must be captured before political power can be sustained.

Instead of direct political confrontation, the method is cultural transformation. It’s about shifting values, language, and accepted norms over time so that certain ideological assumptions become embedded and alternatives are delegitimised or excluded.

Key Pillars of the March

1. Education Systems

Universities, schools, and curriculum bodies are central. Ideological uniformity in academic faculties, especially in humanities and social sciences, has created monocultures where dissent is rare and often punished. Ideas like gender fluidity, systemic oppression, and climate alarmism are taught as settled facts, not debated issues.

2. Media and Narrative Control

Legacy media, especially publicly funded broadcasters, often amplify progressive causes, while downplaying or mocking dissenting views. Entertainment and news media shape culture by framing issues in ways that subtly (or overtly) promote left-leaning interpretations of events.

3. Government and Bureaucracy

Public institutions increasingly adopt DEI (Diversity, Equity, Inclusion) mandates, target-based recruitment, and enforced speech codes. Meritocracy gives way to identity quotas. Laws are introduced or interpreted to criminalise offence or disagreement, particularly on matters of race, gender, and identity.

4. Climate and Energy Policy

Climate action has become a quasi-religious imperative in progressive ideology, resulting in energy policies that prioritise symbolism over feasibility. Traditional energy sources are banned or restricted while newer technologies are adopted without sufficient infrastructure.

5. Shifting the Political Centre

As institutions change, political parties—left and right—follow suit. What was radical 10 years ago becomes mainstream today. Political debates become constrained within the new ideological boundaries.

6. Language and Cultural Symbols

By controlling language, ideology can shape reality. New terms are introduced (e.g., “birthing person”), while old ones are retired or deemed offensive. Cultural rituals such as land acknowledgments or pronoun declarations are normalised, reinforcing ideological positions through repetition.

Criticisms and Responses

Supporters see these changes as necessary progress, correcting historical injustices and creating a more inclusive society. Critics see it as ideological capture—replacing one form of orthodoxy with another and silencing alternative perspectives.

The tension is not over values alone but over control: who decides what is taught, shown, enforced, rewarded, and permitted.

Specific Examples from Australia

  • Peter Ridd Case (JCU): Marine physicist sacked for criticising climate science groupthink and lack of academic freedom.
  • Barry Spurr (University of Sydney): Resigned after leaked private emails, raising concerns over privacy and ideological targeting.
  • Safe Schools Program (Victoria): Introduced under Labor government, promoting gender theory to students; critics claim it crosses into indoctrination.
  • National Curriculum Reforms: Curriculum shifts heavily toward themes of colonisation, oppression, and identity; focus on Western heritage and civic values reduced.
  • ABC and SBS Bias: Public broadcasters regularly accused of promoting progressive causes and downplaying or ignoring conservative viewpoints.
  • Australia Council for the Arts: Funding often goes to activist-driven art projects; traditionalist works underrepresented.
  • Section 18C Case (Andrew Bolt): Found in breach of law for questioning Indigenous identity claims—highlighting speech restrictions on sensitive topics.
  • Voice to Parliament Referendum (2023): Supported by all major institutions (media, sports, business), yet rejected by a public majority—revealing elite-public disconnect.
  • Victorian Gas Ban: New homes banned from connecting to natural gas from 2024 despite energy reliability concerns.
  • School Strike 4 Climate: Endorsed by unions and school leadership; students encouraged to protest government climate policy.
  • Cricket Australia & Australia Day: Removed Australia Day references from Big Bash League promotions; public backlash followed.
  • AFL Pride Round: Mandatory participation and political messaging embedded in sporting events.
  • Public Service DEI Requirements: Government jobs increasingly require DEI statements and identity targets, affecting hiring and promotion.
